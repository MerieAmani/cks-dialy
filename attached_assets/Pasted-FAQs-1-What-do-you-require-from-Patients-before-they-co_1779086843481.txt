FAQs

1. What do you require from Patients before they come in?

- Hepatitis Bs Ag
- Hepatitis C Ab

- Medical report
- Flow-charts

* All the above documents are compulsory and should be no more than 4 weeks old. These tests should be done at majorly recognized hospitals or laboratories.
Any additional medical summary/reports may be requested by the Doctor.

2. What modes of payment do you accept?
Payment is required prior to each treatment and may be made by SHA and any other accredited insurance cover, all major credit cards, cash, cheques and M-pesa.

3. Will dialysis cure my kidney disease? In cases of sudden (acute) kidney failure, diaiysis may be temporary. However, for chronic kidney disease, it is a lifelong requirement unless a successful kidney transplant is performed

4. Does dialysis hurt? The process itself is not painful, though you may feel temporary discomfort when the needles are placed into your vascular access (fistula or graft). This generally becomes much easier to tolerate over time

5. Can I still work or travel while on dialysis? Yes. Many patients continue to work and travel. Some opt for at-home dialysis, which offers more flexible scheduling and easier accommodation for travel plans

6. What diet and fluid restrictions will I have? Patients are typically required to restrict fluid intake (often to less than 1L daily) and limit the consumption of phosphorus, potassium, sodium, and protein

7. Can I eat or drink during dialysis? Outside food and drinks are us tally restricted in-center, as your care team will help you manage your diet around your treatment slots

8. What preparation is needed before starting hemodialysis? Before hemodialysis can begin, a surgeon must create a secure pathway (an "access") to your bloodstream. There are three primary types:
AV Fistula: The gold standard of access. It connects a natural artery to a vein and requires time to heal and mature before it can be used.
AV Graft: A synthetic tube used if your natural blood vessels are not suitable for a fistula.
Catheter: A temporary option used if dialysis needs to start urgently while a permanent access heals.
